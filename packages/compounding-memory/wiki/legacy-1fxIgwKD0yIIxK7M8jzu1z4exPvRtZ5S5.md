## Summary

Biochemist Nick Lane discusses his theory on the origin of life, focusing on the
chemical reactions between hydrogen and carbon dioxide in hydrothermal vents. He
explores the thermodynamic necessity of membranes, the divergence of bacteria
and archaea, and the transition from prebiotic chemistry to complex cellular
life.

## Key Points

- The reaction between CO2 and hydrogen is exergonic, meaning it naturally
  releases energy, yet requires catalysts to overcome kinetic barriers.
- Life likely began in hydrothermal vents where continuous electrical charges
  across pores drove early biochemistry.
- Bacteria and Archaea share a common ancestor but diverged early due to
  fundamental differences in their membranes and DNA replication mechanisms.
- The 'spark' of life is better understood as a continuous chemical reaction and
  energy flow rather than a single instantaneous event.
- Panspermia is considered an unhelpful hypothesis because it lacks a testable
  chemical framework for how life emerges from a sterile environment.
- The eukaryotic cell (complex life) was a singular, rare event in Earth's
  history that did not occur for 2 billion years after the appearance of
  bacteria.
